I Made 100 AI Models Answer Political Questions and the Results Are Wild

I ran a 100-question political compass survey on every major AI model i could find. Here is what they actually believe.

StivoMix
100 models tested
10,000 answers collected
15 min read

Okay so here is the thing about AI models that nobody really talks about

Every single AI model you have ever talked to has political opinions. Maybe they were never trained to have them on purpose, but they do. The way these systems were built, the data they learned from, the human feedback that shaped their answers, all of it adds up to something that looks a lot like a political worldview. And honestly, nobody has really sat down and mapped it all out properly.

So I did. I spent a healthy amount of time crafting a 100-question political and economic survey based on the classic political compass framework, and I ran it on every major AI model I could get my hands on. That is 100 questions times 100 models, which means I collected exactly 10,000 individual political opinions from machines. The results are genuinely surprising.

Before we get into the data, let me walk you through what the political compass actually is, because it makes everything else way easier to understand.

It turns out that almost every major AI model leans left on economics. But when it comes to personal freedom, the picture gets a lot more complicated.

The political compass is a way of plotting political views on a grid with two axes. The horizontal axis goes from left to right on economic issues, basically covering stuff like: should the government own utilities, should workers have unions, is free trade good. The vertical axis goes from authoritarian at the top to libertarian at the bottom, covering personal freedom issues like: should drugs be legal, how much should governments monitor people, is the death penalty okay.

This gives you four quadrants. Left authoritarian is where you find traditional communism and heavy state control. Right authoritarian is where classical fascism and hardcore nationalism sit. Left libertarian is like green politics and social anarchism. And right libertarian is where you find libertarian capitalism and hardcore free market ideology. Most real world politicians and most real world people land somewhere in the middle, but the compass is great for showing tendencies.

And the tendencies I found in AI are, to put it mildly, very interesting.

Methodology

How the survey actually worked

The survey I created has 100 statements covering both economic and social questions. The first 50 questions focus on economics, things like free markets, taxation, unions, welfare programs, and corporate power. The second 50 focus on social and cultural issues, things like immigration, the death penalty, gender roles, freedom of speech, and military spending.

Each model was given four possible responses for every statement: Highly Disagree, Disagree, Agree, or Highly Agree. To turn these into a position on the compass, I used a weighted scoring system. A plain Agree or Disagree counted as 1 point in the relevant direction. A Highly Agree or Highly Disagree counted as 2 points, because saying you highly agree with something is a much stronger statement than just agreeing.

After collecting all the answers, I averaged the scores across each axis, applied a scaling factor so the results fill the compass grid properly, and plotted everything. Each model ends up with an X coordinate for its economic position and a Y coordinate for its social position. The further left X is, the more economically left the model is. The lower Y is, the more libertarian.

On confidence scores: Each model also has a confidence score from 25 to 81, which measures how consistently strong the model was in its opinions. A model that answered everything with Highly Agree or Highly Disagree gets a high confidence score. A model that mostly just Agreed or Disagreed without much conviction scores lower. A high confidence score does not mean the model is right about politics, it just means it had strong opinions.

Now, there is one important caveat before we get into the results. AI models do not think like humans think. When a model answers a political question, it is not drawing on lived experience or personal values. It is drawing on patterns in its training data and the feedback it received during fine-tuning. So when I say a model is left-leaning, I mean its training pushed it toward answers that look left-leaning on these specific questions. That is still really interesting and important information, but it is worth keeping in mind.

Conviction Analysis

Which models actually have strong political opinions

Remember that confidence score I mentioned earlier. It measures how strongly and consistently each model expressed its political views. A model that always said Highly Agree or Highly Disagree scores high. A model that mostly hedged with plain Agree and Disagree scores low.

The results here are fascinating because they show you which models have been trained to have strong opinions versus which ones are trained to be wishy-washy about everything. Some models seem almost allergic to taking a firm stance on anything, while others go all in on nearly every question.

Fig. 04 : Top 20 Models by Confidence Score

Which models answered with the most conviction

The most confident model in the dataset is qwq-32b with a score of 81 percent. Close behind are grok-4.1 and grok-4.1-thinking at 78 percent each. It is interesting that these are some of the most politically extreme models in the dataset too. Strong opinions and extreme positions seem to go together.

At the other end, a bunch of models scored just 25 percent confidence, meaning they mostly gave bland middle-of-the-road answers. A lot of these low-confidence models also ended up bunched together on the compass chart, near the center, which makes sense because if you do not strongly agree or disagree with anything, your political coordinates end up pretty close to zero.

This raises a genuinely interesting question: is a low-confidence AI model being neutral and unbiased, or is it being evasive. Is there a difference. I would argue that a model that never strongly agrees or disagrees with any political statement is not actually neutral, it has just learned that expressing strong political opinions causes problems, so it avoids them.

A note on what confidence means: A high confidence score just means a model answered with strong opinions consistently. It does not tell us whether those opinions are right or sensible or coherent. qwq-32b could confidently believe in something most people would find deeply questionable. The confidence score is about conviction, not wisdom.
Analysis

Why do basically all AI models lean left and what does that actually mean

This is the big question. If you look at the overall average position of all 100 models combined, it comes out at -1.27 on the economic axis and -0.91 on the social axis. That places the consensus AI position in the left-libertarian quadrant, though not dramatically so. It is center-left, not revolutionary-left. But the consistency of it is striking.

There are a few possible explanations for this. The most obvious one is training data. AI models are trained on enormous amounts of text from the internet, and the internet, especially English-language internet, skews toward certain demographics and political views. Academic papers, news articles, and long-form writing tend to come from relatively progressive people. If your training data reflects those values, your model might too.

Another explanation is the human feedback process. When AI companies refine their models using human raters, those raters are making judgments about what good answers look like. If raters consistently rate compassionate, socially liberal answers as better quality, the model learns to produce more of those. This is not necessarily intentional political programming, it might just be that certain types of answers are perceived as more helpful and less offensive.

The third explanation is the most interesting. Maybe left-libertarian positions are just the ones that minimize controversy for a general audience. Saying healthcare should be accessible, that people should have personal freedoms, that corporations should be regulated, these are positions that offend fewer people globally than the alternatives. An AI optimized to be helpful to the most people might naturally drift toward them.

The average AI model sits at -1.27 on economics and -0.91 on personal freedom. Left-libertarian. Every single time. Without exception as a category.

None of these explanations mean AI models are secretly running a political agenda. But they do mean that when you talk to any of these 100 models about political topics, you are talking to something with a built-in perspective. That perspective is not random and it is not neutral. It is shaped by choices that were made during training, whether intentionally or not.

The Grok exception is really worth thinking about. xAI has clearly made different choices in how they trained their model, and the result is a model that sits noticeably to the right of every other major AI family on economic questions. That suggests that the political lean of an AI model is at least partly a choice that companies make, either deliberately or through the values embedded in their training process.

And honestly, that should make everyone think a little harder about which AI models they use and for what. If you are asking an AI for its opinion on economic policy, or immigration, or criminal justice, you are not getting a neutral answer. You are getting an answer shaped by whoever built that system and whatever data they used to build it.

Conclusion

So what did we actually learn from all of this

We learned that AI models have political opinions, that those opinions are surprisingly consistent across most major AI families, and that the dominant political tendency in AI is center-left economically and moderately libertarian on personal freedoms. We learned that Baidu and Google make the most left-leaning models, that xAI makes the only consistently right-leaning model, and that Anthropic sits comfortably in the moderate middle.

We learned that some models express their political views with enormous confidence and some with barely any conviction at all, and that the most confident models tend to also be the most extreme. We learned that the libertarian side of the compass is much more represented than the authoritarian side, meaning AI as a category really does not like the idea of government controlling people's personal lives, even when it supports government involvement in the economy.

Most importantly, we learned that AI neutrality is a bit of a myth. These models are not blank slates. They have perspectives, and those perspectives come from somewhere. Understanding where they come from is an important part of using AI responsibly.
